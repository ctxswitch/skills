## Positioning Method

1. Name the target customer.
2. Name the problem or unmet need.
3. Choose the category or frame of reference.
4. State the value proposition.
5. Name the differentiated mechanism or belief.
6. Attach reasons to believe.
7. Translate the positioning into usable message pillars.
8. Define what the brand should not say.

Best practices:

- Make the category clear before differentiating.
- Use differentiation the buyer values, not only what the company finds interesting.
- Keep proof close to the differentiated claim.
- Include a useful enemy: old way, incumbent, manual workaround, false belief, or avoidable risk.
- Make the statement narrow enough to guide actual copy.

Anti-patterns:

- "For everyone who needs growth."
- Positioning based on adjectives: faster, better, easier, smarter.
- A category so broad that the buyer cannot compare options.
- Differentiation no one can verify.
- Voice traits without examples.

## Positioning Inputs

Collect:

- target customer or industry
- buyer role and user role
- unmet need
- current workaround
- direct and indirect alternatives
- category language buyers already use
- unique value proposition
- differentiation from competitors
- reasons to believe
- origin story if it supports trust
- claims to avoid
- compliance or legal constraints

Competitive position prompts:

- What do competitors lead with?
- What proof do they use?
- What buyer do they ignore?
- What promise do they make but fail to substantiate?
- Where are they vague about implementation, price, support, or risk?
- What can this brand say credibly that others cannot?

## Benefit Ladder

Use the ladder to prevent both feature dumping and empty emotional copy:

1. **Attributes/features**: what the product or service has.
2. **Functional benefits**: what those attributes help the buyer do.
3. **Operational benefits**: what improves in workflow, speed, accuracy, visibility, or coordination.
4. **Psychological/emotional benefits**: what anxiety, confidence, status, or relief changes.
5. **Values and beliefs**: what the brand and buyer agree matters.
6. **Higher-order benefit**: concise strategic promise.

Example pattern:

```markdown
Feature:
Functional benefit:
Operational consequence:
Emotional consequence:
Higher-order promise:
Proof:
```

Rules:

- Do not lead with the top of the ladder unless the lower rungs are obvious or already established.
- Use functional benefits in decision-stage copy.
- Use emotional benefits when the stakes are real and the claim is evidenced.

## Claim Hierarchy

Organize messaging:

- **Primary claim**: the main promise.
- **Support claims**: mechanisms or benefits that make the primary claim believable.
- **Proof claims**: evidence that grounds the support claims.
- **Boundary claims**: what the product is not, who it is not for, or what caveats apply.

Claim test:

- Is it specific?
- Is it important to the buyer?
- Is it different from alternatives?
- Is it provable?
- Is it short enough to remember?

## Brand Voice Method

Voice inputs:

- how the brand should make people feel
- another brand with similar voice
- voices that would repel the target audience
- what makes the voice different from competitors
- language the brand wants to use
- language the brand avoids
- exactly three or four voice traits

Voice chart:

```markdown
Trait:
Description:
Do:
Don't:
Off-brand example:
On-brand rewrite:
```

Voice rules:

- A trait without a do/don't rule is not usable.
- A voice guide without examples will drift.
- "Human" is not enough; define what kind of human.
- Voice should survive format changes: page, email, support, social, error message.

## Reader Relationship

Choose the relationship before tone:

- **Mentor**: experienced, plainspoken, constructive.
- **Peer**: practical, direct, no posturing.
- **Host**: warm, orienting, reassuring.
- **Expert witness**: precise, evidenced, restrained.
- **Challenger**: sharp, opinionated, useful friction.
- **Operator**: concise, implementation-aware, low-drama.

Relationship mismatch examples:

- Expert witness voice with playful social slang creates trust friction.
- Challenger voice without proof feels arrogant.
- Host voice on urgent error copy can feel evasive.
- Peer voice with legal claims can sound underqualified.

## Tone by Context

Voice stays stable; tone shifts.

Sales/pricing:

- confident
- concrete
- risk-aware
- low-pressure

Support/error messages:

- calm
- direct
- next-step oriented
- no jokes at the user's expense

Thought leadership:

- opinionated
- evidenced
- specific about tradeoffs
- avoids inflated certainty

Social:

- compressed
- timely
- conversational
- still on-brand

Compliance-sensitive copy:

- exact
- caveated
- reviewed
- no dramatic promises

## Lexicon and House Rules

Define:

- words to embrace
- banned cliches
- terms competitors overuse
- product capitalization
- numerals
- punctuation preferences
- emoji policy
- humor boundaries
- inclusive language rules
- terms requiring legal or SME review

Lexicon warning:

- Do not ban useful buyer language just because it feels common.
- Do ban phrases that inflate claims, blur category, or make the brand sound interchangeable.

## Review Rubric

Score 0-2:

- Category clarity
- Target customer specificity
- Differentiation
- Reasons to believe
- Benefit ladder completeness
- Voice trait usability
- Context-sensitive tone
- Lexicon consistency
- Claims to avoid

## Common Findings

Positioning too broad:

- Evidence: copy could address many unrelated buyers.
- Fix: specify segment, trigger, and use case.

Differentiation by adjective:

- Evidence: "better/faster/easier" without mechanism.
- Fix: name mechanism and proof.

Voice without guardrails:

- Evidence: traits listed but no examples.
- Fix: add do/don't and before/after rewrites.

Emotional overreach:

- Evidence: high-level promise unsupported by workflow proof.
- Fix: step down benefit ladder and rebuild.

Category confusion:

- Evidence: reader cannot tell what kind of solution this is.
- Fix: use buyer's category language earlier.
