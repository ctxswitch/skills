# Positioning and Voice

Use this reference for brand messaging, voice guides, tone adjustments, positioning statements, and edits that need to sound like a coherent brand rather than generic marketing prose.

## Positioning Inputs

Answer these before writing positioning-heavy copy:

- target customer or industry
- unmet need or problem
- category or frame of reference
- unique value proposition
- differentiation from competitors
- reasons to believe
- current positioning and competitor positioning
- origin story or mission when it matters to trust
- existing taglines or banned claims

Positioning statement shape:

```markdown
For [target customer],
[brand] is the [category]
that delivers [value proposition]
unlike [alternative or competitor],
because [reason to believe].
```

## Benefit Ladder

Move from concrete to motivating:

1. **Attributes/features**: what the product or service has.
2. **Functional benefits**: what those attributes help the buyer do.
3. **Psychological/emotional benefits**: how the buyer feels or what risk is reduced.
4. **Values and beliefs**: what the brand and buyer agree matters.
5. **Higher-order benefit**: the concise strategic promise.

Do not skip the lower rungs. Emotional copy without functional support reads hollow.

## Brand Voice

Useful voice inputs:

- how the brand should make people feel
- another brand with a similar voice
- voices that would repel the target audience
- what makes the voice different from competitors
- language the brand wants to use
- exactly three or four adjectives that define the voice

Voice chart:

```markdown
Trait:
Description:
Do:
Don't:
Example before:
Example after:
```

## Reader Relationship

Choose the relationship before choosing tone:

- **Mentor**: experienced, constructive, plainspoken.
- **Peer**: practical, direct, no posturing.
- **Host**: warm, orienting, reassuring.
- **Expert witness**: precise, evidence-led, restrained.
- **Challenger**: sharp, opinionated, useful friction.

## Tone by Context

Voice stays stable; tone shifts with the situation.

- Sales/pricing: concrete, confident, low-pressure.
- Support/error messages: calm, useful, direct.
- Thought leadership: opinionated but evidenced.
- Social: compressed, human, context-aware.
- Compliance-sensitive copy: restrained, exact, reviewable.

## Lexicon Rules

Maintain:

- words and phrases the brand embraces
- banned or tired cliches
- capitalization and product-name rules
- numerals, punctuation, and formatting preferences
- emoji or humor policy

Avoid one-note adjectives. Voice becomes usable when it includes examples and anti-examples.
