## SEO Content Method

1. Identify the primary query or topic.
2. Classify intent.
3. Review the expected SERP format and reader task.
4. Define the brand's unique point of view.
5. Build a brief before drafting.
6. Create an outline that answers the task and supports business intent.
7. Add evidence, internal links, CTA, and measurement.
8. Review for usefulness, not only keyword coverage.

Best practices:

- Match search intent before optimizing phrasing.
- Add original value beyond the existing SERP.
- Use headings as navigational promises.
- Tie informational content to a logical next step.
- Use internal links to connect educational and commercial pages.

Anti-patterns:

- Keyword list with no reader task.
- Generic intro that delays the answer.
- Outline copied from top-ranking pages without a point of view.
- FAQ stuffing.
- CTA unrelated to the reader's stage.
- Measuring a new article only by final conversion.

## Intent Classification

Intent types:

- **Know**: explain, define, understand.
- **Do**: complete a task, use a template, calculate, configure.
- **Compare**: alternatives, pros/cons, best tools, vendor comparison.
- **Buy**: pricing, demo, implementation, reviews, service pages.
- **Local/Go**: find a specific brand, product, page, or location.

Stage mapping:

- Awareness: Know, broad problem, beginner guide.
- Consideration: Compare, methods, templates, alternatives.
- Decision: Buy, pricing, demo, proof, risk reducers.
- Retention: Do, setup, advanced use, troubleshooting.

Intent mismatch signs:

- Reader wants a template but gets a thought piece.
- Reader wants comparison but gets product pitch.
- Reader wants pricing but gets vague value copy.
- Reader wants a quick definition but gets a long origin story.

## SERP Review

Look for:

- dominant content format
- depth expectation
- freshness needs
- featured snippet pattern
- People Also Ask questions
- comparison dimensions
- entities and related concepts
- common examples
- missing perspectives
- weak competitor claims

Do not blindly copy SERP structure. Use it to understand reader expectations, then add a better angle.

## Unique Point of View

Answer:

- What can this brand say that a generic article cannot?
- What has the team seen in real customers?
- What tradeoff does the category usually hide?
- What implementation detail changes the advice?
- What data, template, checklist, or example can be added?

Strong POV sources:

- first-party data
- customer stories
- operational method
- field experience
- benchmarks
- examples
- failure modes
- contrarian but evidenced stance
- industry-specific constraints

Weak POV:

- "X is important"
- "Here are 10 tips"
- "Use our product"
- "In today's landscape"

## Outline Design

Good outline:

- answers the query early
- uses headings that make sense while scanning
- moves from basic understanding to decision support
- includes examples or templates where the reader needs application
- handles objections or edge cases
- points to the next useful action

Outline skeleton:

```markdown
H1:
Intro job:
Direct answer:
H2:
  H3:
  H3:
H2:
  H3:
Examples:
Common mistakes:
FAQ:
CTA:
```

Heading test:

- Does each heading make a promise?
- Is the promise useful to the reader?
- Does the section fulfill it directly?

## On-Page Essentials

Title tag:

- roughly 50-60 characters when practical
- aligns with intent
- includes differentiator if space allows

Meta description:

- summarize value
- include next step or useful hook
- avoid keyword stuffing

Internal links:

- to pillar pages
- from related articles
- to relevant product/commercial pages
- to proof assets

Schema:

- use only if justified by content type
- FAQ, how-to, article, product, review, event, or local as appropriate

Images:

- use diagrams, screenshots, process visuals, comparison tables, examples
- avoid decorative stock images when the reader needs clarity

## Evidence and Compliance

Track:

- data points
- source links
- expert quotes
- customer examples
- screenshots
- compliance caveats
- claims needing SME review
- update dates

E-E-A-T practical checks:

- Does the article show real experience?
- Is advice specific enough to act on?
- Are claims sourced or caveated?
- Is the author or reviewer credible for the topic?
- Does the content avoid pretending certainty where there is none?

## Measurement

Pick a leading metric:

- indexed and crawled
- ranking movement
- impressions
- clicks
- engaged sessions
- scroll depth
- internal link clicks
- newsletter signups
- template downloads
- trial starts
- demo assists

Review windows:

- Early: indexing, impressions, query shape.
- Middle: clicks, engagement, ranking movement.
- Later: assisted conversion, internal link flow, refresh needs.

## Common Findings

Intent mismatch:

- Evidence: format does not match reader task.
- Fix: change asset type or restructure opening.

No POV:

- Evidence: article repeats existing SERP.
- Fix: add field insight, example, template, or tradeoff.

Weak intro:

- Evidence: delays answer with generic setup.
- Fix: answer directly, then add context.

Commercial dead end:

- Evidence: useful article has no logical next step.
- Fix: add CTA based on stage.

Keyword stuffing:

- Evidence: repeated phrase hurts readability.
- Fix: use natural language and related entities.
