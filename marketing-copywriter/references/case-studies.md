## Case Study Method

1. Choose one story outcome.
2. Identify the business problem and status quo.
3. Capture why the customer changed now.
4. Show the decision path and alternatives.
5. Explain implementation without turning the story into a feature list.
6. Present results with timeframe and caveats.
7. Use quotes that sound like the customer.
8. End with a CTA tied to a similar reader's next step.

Best practices:

- Use tension before resolution.
- Keep the customer as protagonist.
- Tie vendor actions to customer outcomes.
- Use specific before/after evidence.
- Include caveats where they increase trust.

Anti-patterns:

- Vendor hero story disguised as customer story.
- "Challenge, solution, results" with no tension or decision detail.
- Metrics without timeframe.
- Quotes that sound like approved brochure copy.
- Too many product features and not enough operational change.

## Story Selection

Good case study candidates have:

- clear before/after contrast
- measurable outcome or credible qualitative shift
- relatable status quo pain
- decision tension
- implementation detail
- buyer or practitioner quote
- permission to use enough specifics

Weak candidates:

- customer likes the product but cannot name a result
- no permission for metrics or quotes
- story depends on confidential details
- outcome is too broad to connect to the product/service
- implementation is atypical and may mislead prospects

## Interview Flow

For a 45-60 minute call:

1. **Context**: company, role, team, baseline situation.
2. **Status quo pain**: time lost, risk, revenue leakage, customer friction, team frustration.
3. **Trigger**: what made the old way unacceptable.
4. **Decision**: why they chose this solution over alternatives or inertia.
5. **Implementation**: what changed, what surprised them, what support mattered.
6. **Results**: before/after numbers, timeframe, caveats.
7. **Forward look**: how they use it now, what comes next, referral willingness.

Interview posture:

- Ask for examples, not adjectives.
- Ask "what happened next?" to find causality.
- Ask "how did you know?" to find proof.
- Ask "what nearly stopped this?" to find objections.
- Ask "how would you describe it to a peer?" to get usable quotes.

## Probe Bank

Use sparingly:

- What would have happened if you had not changed?
- What almost stopped you from moving ahead?
- What did your team stop doing after implementation?
- What work became visible for the first time?
- What surprised you during rollout?
- What did your boss or team care about most?
- Which result mattered most internally?
- What would you tell someone comparing alternatives?
- What caveat should a skeptical reader know?
- What is different now that would be hard to give up?

## Story Spine

Default shape:

1. **Situation**: brief context.
2. **Complication**: what was going wrong and why it mattered.
3. **Decision**: why the customer chose this path.
4. **Resolution**: what changed through implementation.
5. **Evidence**: metrics, quote, artifact, before/after.
6. **Forward look**: how the customer now operates.

Narrative rules:

- Open with the result if the proof is strong.
- Open with the pain if the reader needs context.
- Avoid chronological detail that does not change belief.
- Use implementation details that answer buyer objections.

## Evidence Handling

Evidence types:

- quantitative metrics
- time saved
- revenue or pipeline impact
- cost avoided
- risk reduced
- adoption rate
- quote
- screenshot
- artifact
- external validation

Metric rules:

- Include timeframe.
- Include baseline.
- Note caveats.
- Validate owner.
- Avoid implying causation if the source does not support it.

Metric pattern:

```markdown

```

## Quote Capture

Gold-standard quote:

- short
- specific
- attributable
- sounds like a person
- includes a result, contrast, or emotional truth

Weak quote:

- repeats vendor tagline
- uses generic praise
- contains three abstractions
- sounds like PR or legal wrote it

Quote rescue prompt:

```markdown
Can you say that again the way you would text a colleague?
```

## Approval and Risk

Before publication:

- metric owner validated data
- caveats noted
- quote sign-off complete
- logo usage confirmed
- sensitive data redacted
- competitor names approved or removed
- legal disclaimer added if needed
- past-performance claims reviewed
- regulated industry claims reviewed

Approval risk:

- The more polished the quote, the less believable it may become.
- Preserve human phrasing while meeting approval constraints.

## Common Findings

No tension:

- Evidence: story jumps from "needed solution" to "implemented solution."
- Fix: add status quo cost and decision pressure.

Vendor-centered:

- Evidence: company actions dominate customer experience.
- Fix: rewrite around customer problem, decision, and outcome.

Unsupported metric:

- Evidence: result lacks baseline or timeframe.
- Fix: add caveat or use qualitative proof.

Quote sounds fake:

- Evidence: quote uses brand adjectives or product tagline.
- Fix: shorten and make conversational.

CTA disconnected:

- Evidence: case study ends without next action.
- Fix: point similar reader to relevant demo, guide, comparison, or consultation.
