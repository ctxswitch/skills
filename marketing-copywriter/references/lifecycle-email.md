# Lifecycle Email

Use this reference for welcome sequences, onboarding, email nurture, newsletters, subject lines, and email copy reviews.

## Principles

Email works when it respects attention and intent:

- one main idea per message
- one clear next step
- useful rhythm rather than constant selling
- trust preserved over the sequence, not burned in one send

## Onboarding Arc

Default sequence:

1. **Deliver the promise**: what they get, how often, how to whitelist or start.
2. **Quick win**: one tip, template, resource, setup action, or result they can use today.
3. **Belief shift**: short story, metric, proof point, or case fragment that reframes the problem.
4. **Depth**: pillar content, product education, or segment-specific path.
5. **Soft conversion**: demo, reply, low-friction offer, consult, trial step, or product action.

Do not ask for a high-friction conversion before trust or intent supports it.

## Nurture Lanes

Rotate lanes so the sequence does not become repetitive:

- **Educational lane**: teach something specific. CTA = read, watch, use, check, compare.
- **Proof lane**: customer outcome, quote, benchmark, analyst mention, example. CTA = see case, book call, view proof.
- **Product lane**: feature tied to outcome. CTA = try, compare, configure, talk to sales.

Avoid three consecutive emails that feel like the same ask.

## Newsletter Rhythm

Structure:

- subject line earns the open without false urgency
- opening hook follows through on the subject
- single thesis: what the reader will think or do differently
- useful body: story, insight, example, or tool
- sign-off in a consistent voice
- optional P.S. for a secondary link or human touch

## Subject Line Hygiene

Prefer specificity over hype:

- Better: `How finance teams cut month-end review time`
- Worse: `You won't believe this`

Avoid false urgency, fake personalization, bait-and-switch curiosity, and all-caps pressure unless the brand and situation truly justify it.

## Compliance and Operations

For commercial email, flag:

- unsubscribe or preference center
- privacy-policy alignment
- legitimate-interest or consent assumptions where relevant
- claims that need legal, compliance, or product review
