## Core Copy Concepts

**Copy**

- Persuasive or action-oriented text, usually tied to a business goal.
- Examples: landing page hero, ad, CTA, email, product page, sales page.

**Content**

- Informational, educational, entertaining, or trust-building material.
- Content can sell indirectly, but its immediate job is often understanding, engagement, or relationship.

**Marketing prose**

- Broad term for business-facing writing that needs to inform, persuade, position, or guide.
- Includes copy, content, campaign messaging, product messaging, and thought leadership.

**Claim**

- A statement the reader must believe for the copy to work.
- Strong claims need proof, caveats, or a clearer mechanism.

**Mechanism**

- The specific way the product, service, or process creates the promised outcome.
- Mechanism prevents copy from becoming empty promise.

**Offer**

- The thing being presented for action.
- Can be a product, service, lead magnet, demo, trial, consultation, event, or content asset.

**CTA**

- Call to action.
- Should specify the action and, when useful, what happens next.

**Microcopy**

- Small interface or conversion text: button labels, form hints, error messages, reassurance text, unsubscribe language.
- Often carries trust and conversion weight.

## Audience and Awareness

**Audience**

- The group the copy is written for.
- Useful only when specific enough to guide word choice, proof, and CTA.

**Buyer**

- Person or group making the purchase decision.
- May differ from the user.

**User**

- Person who uses the product or service.
- User proof may not persuade an executive buyer unless connected to business impact.

**Approver**

- Person who can block or approve the purchase, often legal, finance, security, procurement, or leadership.

**Stage of awareness**

- How much the reader understands about their problem and options.
- Unaware: does not recognize problem.
- Problem-aware: recognizes pain but not solution.
- Solution-aware: knows solution category.
- Product-aware: knows the brand/product.
- Most-aware: ready to act and needs final proof or offer.

**Trigger**

- Event that makes the reader care now.
- Examples: missed target, audit, launch, churn spike, migration, new hire, budget cycle, customer complaint.

**Jobs-to-be-done**

- The progress the buyer is trying to make in a specific situation.
- Includes functional, social, emotional, and switching jobs.

## Positioning and Messaging

**Positioning**

- How the brand/product should be understood relative to alternatives in the buyer's mind.
- Defines category, audience, value, differentiation, and reasons to believe.

**Messaging**

- The repeatable claims, pillars, proof, and language used to express positioning.

**Value proposition**

- The specific value the buyer gets and why it matters.
- Should connect outcome, audience, and differentiation.

**Differentiation**

- A meaningful reason the buyer should choose this option over alternatives.
- Needs to be valued by the buyer and supported by proof.

**Category**

- The frame of reference buyers use to compare options.
- If category is unclear, differentiation usually does not land.

**Message pillar**

- A major supporting theme under the core positioning.
- Should include claim, mechanism, proof, and likely objection.

**Tagline**

- Short brand or campaign phrase.
- Usually not enough to carry conversion copy by itself.

## Proof and Persuasion

**Proof**

- Evidence that supports a claim.
- Can be metric, quote, logo, testimonial, case study, demo, screenshot, benchmark, method, certification, or implementation detail.

**Reason to believe**

- The proof or mechanism that makes a positioning claim credible.

**Social proof**

- Evidence that other people or organizations trust the product.
- Stronger when specific to the buyer's segment or use case.

**Risk reversal**

- Copy or offer element that reduces perceived risk.
- Examples: trial, guarantee, migration support, cancellation policy, security docs, transparent caveat.

**Objection**

- Reason the reader hesitates.
- Good copy handles objections before or near the decision point.

**Belief shift**

- The change in understanding needed before the reader will act.
- Useful for emails, landing pages, and thought leadership.

## Conversion Vocabulary

**Conversion**

- The desired action, not always a purchase.
- Examples: demo request, trial start, download, reply, signup, pricing view.

**Primary CTA**

- The main action the page or email should drive.

**Secondary CTA**

- Lower-friction action for qualified hesitation.
- Should not compete with the primary CTA unless intentionally serving a different stage.

**Friction**

- Anything that makes action harder: unclear value, too many fields, missing proof, risk, effort, timing, privacy concern.

**Above the fold**

- First visible area before scrolling.
- Must establish relevance, category, value, and next action quickly.

**Hero**

- Primary top section of a landing page or homepage.
- Usually includes headline, subhead, CTA, support text, and visual/proof.

**Objection handling**

- Copy that answers likely hesitation: price, switching, setup, security, support, results, timing.

## Content and SEO Vocabulary

**Search intent**

- What the searcher is trying to accomplish.
- Know, Do, Compare, Buy, Local, or Go.

**SEO content brief**

- Instructions for creating search-focused content.
- Should include query, intent, reader, POV, outline, evidence, links, CTA, and measurement.

**SERP**

- Search engine results page.
- Review it to understand expected formats and missing opportunities.

**Pillar page**

- Broad authoritative page for a topic cluster.

**Topic cluster**

- Related content around a core topic connected by internal links.

**Meta description**

- Search-result summary.
- Should create useful expectation and reason to click, not stuff keywords.

**Schema**

- Structured data that helps search engines understand page type.
- Use when content justifies it.

## Email Vocabulary

**Lifecycle email**

- Email tied to customer/subscriber stage or behavior.

**Onboarding sequence**

- Early emails that orient the reader, deliver value, and guide first meaningful action.

**Nurture sequence**

- Emails that build understanding, belief, proof, and readiness over time.

**Newsletter**

- Recurring editorial email.
- Should have a recognizable rhythm and reason to open.

**Subject line**

- Inbox headline.
- Must earn the open honestly.

**Preview text**

- Inbox support copy after the subject.
- Should add context, not repeat the subject.

## Brand Voice Vocabulary

**Voice**

- Stable personality and writing character of the brand.

**Tone**

- Situational expression of voice.
- Tone changes by context, urgency, channel, and reader emotion.

**Lexicon**

- Words and phrases the brand uses or avoids.

**House rules**

- Formatting and style choices: capitalization, numerals, punctuation, emoji, product names.

**Reader relationship**

- The role the brand takes with the reader: mentor, peer, host, expert witness, challenger, operator.

## Common Distinctions

**Benefit vs feature**

- Feature: what it has.
- Benefit: what the reader gets.
- Strong benefit: what changes in the reader's workflow, risk, status, or outcome.

**Outcome vs output**

- Output: thing produced.
- Outcome: useful change created by the output.

**Proof vs claim**

- Claim asks for belief.
- Proof earns belief.

**Positioning vs copy**

- Positioning defines strategy.
- Copy expresses it in a specific asset and moment.

**Voice vs tone**

- Voice is stable.
- Tone adapts.

**Objection vs FAQ**

- Objection blocks action.
- FAQ may simply answer information needs.
- High-impact objections deserve more than a buried FAQ.

**CTA vs next step**

- CTA is the visible prompt.
- Next step is what actually happens after the click.
- Good copy aligns both.

## Words to Handle Carefully

Avoid or substantiate:

- seamless
- robust
- innovative
- scalable
- game-changing
- world-class
- cutting-edge
- effortless
- guaranteed
- best
- leading
- secure
- compliant
- AI-powered
- personalized
- end-to-end

These words are not banned. They need specificity, mechanism, or proof.

Replacement pattern:

```markdown
Weak word:
Specific mechanism:
Buyer outcome:
Proof:
Rewrite:
```
