# Research and Proof

Use this before writing copy from scratch, reviewing vague copy, or building a brief. The goal is to turn buyer evidence into useful claims, objections, proof points, and page/email direction.

## Research Method

1. State the asset and desired action.
2. Identify the reader's stage: unaware, problem-aware, solution-aware, product-aware, or ready to act.
3. Capture the buyer trigger that makes the topic urgent now.
4. Extract words the buyer uses from calls, reviews, tickets, comments, search queries, or prior emails.
5. Name the status quo and the cost of staying there.
6. Compare competitor claims and find the gap this brand can credibly own.
7. Inventory proof before drafting claims.
8. Turn the research into a brief with message hierarchy, objections, proof, and CTA.

Best practices:

- Start from actual buyer language, then tighten it.
- Keep claims proportional to proof.
- Use research to remove copy, not only to add copy.
- Put the highest-friction objection near the conversion point.
- Mark unknowns as placeholders instead of filling them with generic claims.

Anti-patterns:

- Writing benefit bullets before naming the buyer's trigger.
- Differentiating with adjectives instead of evidence.
- Using competitor sameness as proof that a phrase is safe.
- Treating a persona as demographics rather than motivation and context.
- Treating keyword volume as intent.

## Buyer Insight

Capture:

- **Segment**: who the copy is for.
- **Role**: buyer, user, approver, practitioner, executive sponsor.
- **Trigger**: event that makes them look now.
- **Current workaround**: spreadsheet, agency, manual process, incumbent tool, internal team, doing nothing.
- **Desired outcome**: what changes if this works.
- **Risk**: what they fear losing, breaking, wasting, or exposing.
- **Decision pressure**: deadline, budget, audit, growth goal, launch, migration, complaint, churn.

Do not flatten all audiences into one "customer." A CFO, operator, developer, and founder may buy the same product for different reasons and need different proof.

## Jobs-to-Be-Done Prompts

Functional job:

- What task are they trying to complete?
- What workflow is slow, risky, invisible, or expensive?
- What decision do they need to make?

Social job:

- Who needs to trust their choice?
- What do they need to look competent, careful, strategic, or innovative about?
- What embarrassment or political risk are they avoiding?

Emotional job:

- What anxiety does the current state create?
- What would make them feel in control?
- What frustration are they tired of explaining?

Switching job:

- What are they firing?
- Why is the old way no longer good enough?
- What nearly stopped them from switching?

## Voice-of-Customer Capture

Useful sources:

- sales-call notes
- support tickets
- customer reviews
- onboarding calls
- win/loss notes
- search queries
- community threads
- competitor reviews
- customer emails
- survey open text

Tag phrases by use:

- **Pain**: words that describe the status quo.
- **Outcome**: words that describe the desired state.
- **Objection**: words that explain hesitation.
- **Proof**: words that describe trust signals.
- **Category**: words buyers use for the solution.

Rewrite rule:

- Preserve the buyer's nouns and worries.
- Improve rhythm and clarity.
- Do not sanitize away the emotional or operational specificity.

## Objection Map

Common objections:

- price or budget
- switching cost
- migration risk
- setup time
- security or compliance
- integration uncertainty
- internal approval
- vendor trust
- reliability
- proof of ROI
- "we can build this ourselves"
- "we already tried something like this"

For each objection, define:

```markdown
Objection:
Where it appears:
What the buyer needs to believe:
Proof or risk reducer:
Copy placement:
```

Examples of risk reducers:

- migration plan
- trial
- guarantee
- implementation support
- security page
- compliance badge
- customer proof
- transparent pricing
- calculator
- comparison table
- caveat or honest limitation

## Competitor Lens

Build a compact matrix:

```markdown
Competitor:
Who they sell to:
Core claim:
Proof used:
CTA:
Onboarding story:
Risk reversal:
Tone:
Gap we can own:
```

What to look for:

- repeated headline formulas
- shared category language
- overused proof types
- claims everyone makes but few prove
- missing buyer segments
- hidden objections
- unclear switching stories
- pricing or packaging ambiguity

Planning rule:

- If every competitor says the same thing, decide whether it is a category requirement or a weak convention.
- If it is required, say it more clearly and prove it faster.
- If it is weak, choose a more specific angle.

## Website and Messaging Audit

Five-second test:

- Can a stranger name who it is for?
- Can they name the category?
- Can they name the promised outcome?
- Can they identify the next action?

Page-level checks:

- One primary CTA is obvious.
- Proof appears close to claims.
- The page answers the reader's next objection.
- Jargon is either buyer language or explained.
- Sections follow the reader's decision path.
- The page has a job beyond "explain everything."

Copy friction signs:

- hero copy could fit a competitor
- subhead explains the headline instead of advancing belief
- proof is buried below long claims
- CTA asks for too much too soon
- benefits repeat with different adjectives
- "for everyone" positioning
- no acknowledgement of switching cost

## Search Intent Inputs

Classify target queries:

- **Know**: explain, define, teach.
- **Do**: complete a task, download, configure, calculate.
- **Compare**: evaluate options, alternatives, pros/cons.
- **Buy**: pricing, demo, vendor, implementation, guarantee.
- **Local/Go**: reach a known brand, location, or page.

Map intent to asset:

- Know -> guide, explainer, pillar, glossary.
- Do -> template, checklist, calculator, tool, tutorial.
- Compare -> alternative page, buyer guide, matrix.
- Buy -> product page, pricing page, landing page, case study.

Search warning:

- Ranking content still needs a point of view. Matching SERP shape is not the same as sounding useful or differentiated.

## Proof Inventory

Proof types:

- metrics
- customer logos
- testimonials
- case studies
- screenshots
- demos
- third-party badges
- methodology
- founder/operator experience
- security or compliance artifacts
- implementation details
- before/after examples
- benchmarks

Proof placement:

- Put proof next to the claim it supports.
- Use strong proof for high-belief claims.
- Use lightweight proof for low-risk claims.
- Do not use logos as proof for specific performance unless the logo actually supports that claim.

Claim/proof table:

```markdown
Claim:
Belief required: low / medium / high
Available proof:
Missing proof:
Where proof should appear:
Fallback wording if proof is missing:
```
