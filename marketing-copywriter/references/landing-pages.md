## Landing Page Method

1. Define the primary conversion.
2. Name the visitor and their stage of awareness.
3. State the strongest outcome the page can honestly promise.
4. Explain the mechanism behind the outcome.
5. Match proof to the claims that require belief.
6. Handle the objection most likely to block conversion.
7. Remove sections that do not increase clarity, desire, trust, or action.

Best practices:

- One page, one dominant job.
- Use the visitor's category language before introducing brand language.
- Lead with the buyer's desired outcome, not the company's process.
- Put risk reducers before the reader has to commit.
- Make the CTA describe what happens next.

Anti-patterns:

- Hero headline that is clever but hides the category.
- CTA that asks for a meeting before establishing relevance.
- Feature dump before the reader believes the problem matters.
- Proof wall detached from claims.
- Multiple equal CTAs with different intent.
- FAQ used as a junk drawer for unresolved messaging.

## Narrative Spine

Use this order as the default:

1. **Context**: who this is for and what situation they are in.
2. **Problem**: the cost of the status quo.
3. **Outcome**: the concrete after-state.
4. **Mechanism**: how the offer makes the outcome possible.
5. **Proof**: evidence matched to the claim.
6. **Risk reversal**: trial, guarantee, support, security, compliance, migration, transparent caveat.
7. **Single CTA**: one dominant action, with secondary actions only for qualified hesitation.

Mechanism matters. A promise without a mechanism sounds like hype; a mechanism without an outcome sounds like a feature dump.

## Above the Fold

Hero block:

- **Headline**: category plus outcome, or a promise that still makes the category obvious.
- **Subhead**: who it is for, what changes, or why the approach works.
- **Primary CTA**: verb + object.
- **Support text**: timeframe, risk reducer, or expectation-setting microcopy.
- **Visual**: product, workflow, result, customer proof, or useful diagram.

Headline tests:

- Can the visitor tell what kind of thing this is?
- Can the visitor tell whether it is for them?
- Does it imply a business or personal outcome?
- Does it avoid a claim that needs proof before the proof appears?

Subhead jobs:

- narrow the audience
- explain the mechanism
- make the promise concrete
- reduce risk
- introduce proof

CTA examples:

- `Book a 20-minute fit call`
- `Compare plans`
- `Start a free trial`
- `Get the migration checklist`
- `See the workflow`
- `Calculate your savings`

Weak CTAs:

- `Submit`
- `Learn more`
- `Get started` when the next step is unclear
- `Contact us` when the buyer needs a specific path

## Message Hierarchy

A useful page usually has:

1. Primary promise.
2. Who it is for.
3. Why the status quo costs too much.
4. How the offer works.
5. Why the claim is believable.
6. What objections have been accounted for.
7. What to do next.

If everything is equally important, the page will feel noisy. Choose one primary claim and make all sections support it.

## Body Sections

Common blocks:

- **Problem cost**: quantify or describe the pain of staying still.
- **How it works**: three steps max; each step tied to a user benefit.
- **Benefits**: outcome-led, not feature-labeled.
- **Use cases**: segment-specific moments where the offer fits.
- **Who it is for / not for**: filters bad-fit buyers.
- **Proof stack**: metrics, quotes, logos, screenshots, third-party trust.
- **Comparison**: why this approach vs old way, competitor, internal build, agency, or doing nothing.
- **Objections/FAQ**: price, implementation, security, timeline, ownership, support.
- **Final CTA**: repeat the primary action with clear consequence.

Benefit rewrite pattern:

```markdown
Feature: automated reports
Weak benefit: save time with automated reports
Stronger benefit: close Monday pipeline reviews without chasing five exports
Proof needed: before/after review time or customer quote
```

## Proof and Objection Handling

Proof must answer "why should I believe this?" at the moment belief is needed.

Proof by claim type:

- Speed claim -> before/after time, setup steps, benchmark.
- Revenue claim -> customer metric, caveat, timeframe.
- Trust claim -> security/compliance proof, third-party validation.
- Ease claim -> screenshot, workflow, onboarding detail.
- Differentiation claim -> comparison table, method, unique constraint.

Objection placement:

- Above fold: core relevance and category clarity.
- Middle: mechanism, switching, proof, comparison.
- Near CTA: risk, next step, cost, time commitment, privacy.

FAQ rule:

- Use FAQs for conversion friction, not random information.
- If a question is critical to belief, consider promoting it into the page body.

## CTA Rules

A CTA should tell the visitor:

- what they will do
- what they will get
- how much commitment is involved
- what happens after the click if that is a concern

CTA formula:

```markdown
[Verb] [specific object] [optional expectation]
```

Examples:

- `Book a demo`
- `Get the checklist`
- `See pricing`
- `Start a 14-day trial`
- `Talk to an implementation specialist`

Microcopy examples:

- `No credit card required.`
- `Takes about 3 minutes.`
- `We will send the checklist to your email.`
- `Includes migration questions and security review.`

## Page Review Rubric

Score 0-2:

- Category clarity
- Audience fit
- Outcome specificity
- Mechanism clarity
- Proof proximity
- Objection handling
- CTA clarity
- Voice fit
- Skimmability
- Differentiation

Interpretation:

- 0: missing or generic
- 1: present but weak
- 2: specific, useful, and supported

## Common Findings

Generic hero:

- Evidence: headline could apply to any competitor.
- Fix: include category, audience, and outcome.

Unsupported promise:

- Evidence: strong claim appears before proof.
- Fix: add proof nearby or soften the claim.

CTA mismatch:

- Evidence: page educates early-stage reader but asks for sales call.
- Fix: add lower-friction CTA or strengthen decision-stage proof.

Feature dump:

- Evidence: section lists capabilities without user consequence.
- Fix: translate features into workflow outcomes.

Objection gap:

- Evidence: high-friction issue absent from page.
- Fix: add FAQ, comparison row, proof block, or risk reversal.
